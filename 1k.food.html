Korean Food is a delight for the senses that has gained more exposure in the west in the recent years. Originating from the farming and nomadic traditions in the Korean peninsula, Korean food has evolved throughout classic dynasties and current day modernization. Korean cuisine is primarily centered on rice, vegetables and meat. Traditional Korean meals begin with a series of small side dishes that are eaten as an appetizer and with the main dishes during the meal. Gochujang, the infamous fermented chili paste is a staple flavor among various dishes. Many of the dishes highlighted here have their roots in the Joseon Dynasty, but have endured over centuries and have been reimagined in many newer eateries for modern day taste.



5 Korean Restaurant Finds To Try

Take 31

Founded in December 2011, Take 31 aims to add modern twists to traditional Korean dishes by incorporating the culinary edge of street-food flavors. Every month Take 31, has a 'Soul Food' special, as they term it so one can try a unique dish not always on the menu. While Take 31 is only open for dinner service it is a casual Korean gastropub appealing to locals and the well-traveled, the Korean food curiuos and aficionados. The average price of a dish ranges from $11-30 and they accept major credit cards.

Woorijip

Located in the heart of New York's Koreatown, Woorijip is a Korean Buffet priding itself on its noodle dishes, however serving a little bit of everything Korean food is known for. Woorijip has a hot and cold buffet as well as pre-packaged to-go options making it a great place for lunch and dinner. Additionally, since many of the dishes are vegetable-based, Woorijip has become a great option for vegetarians as well. The average price is around $10 for a dish or a few selections from the buffet and major credit cards are accepted.

Hanbat

With classic wooden decor, Hanbat is a traditional Korean eatery located three blocks north of the main strip in Koreatown. Hanbat is open 24/7 so you can satisfy your Korean cravings anytime of the day! One of their signature dishes is Bibimbap in a hot stone bowl. A secret of Hanbat is that you can order anything from their sister restaurant next door Hyodonggak, so if you are in the mood for Jajangmyeon politely request an order. The average price of a dish ranges from $11-30 and major credit cards are accepted.

Caffe Bene

Unlike the recommendations above, Caffe Bene is a chain with 1600 locations worldwide. Caffe Bene inspired by European cafes started in 2008 in Seoul, South Korea and expanded rapidly over the next 4 years. Caffe Bene offers a fun and inventive menu of brewed coffee, other specialty beverages, Korean pastries, waffles, gelato and patbingsu (Korean shaved ice dessert). The average price of a dish is around $10 and major credit cards are accepted.

5 Popular Korean Dishes To Try

Traditionally eaten on the eve of the lunar New Year, Bibimbap meaning mixed rice, was a dish created as a way to finish off all of the leftover side dishes before the beginning of the New Year. Bibimbap is a colorful dish best served in a hot stone bowl of rice topped with various sliced vegetables, meat and a raw egg (to be cooked as you mix the rice, vegetables and meat in the bowl).
Cool Fact: The colors of Bibimbap symbolize specific parts of the body each ingredient nutritionally supports. Dark or black ingredients like mushrooms represent north for the kidneys. Red or orange ingredients like chili or carrots represent south for the heart. Green ingredients like zucchini represent east for the liver. White ingredients like rice or radish represent west for the lungs. Lastly, yellow ingredients like the egg yolk represent center for the stomach.

Bibimbap

Bulgogi meaning fire meat refers to marinated meat (typically beef) that is grilled over special grills. During the Joseon Dynasty bulgogi was traditionally prepared particularly for royalty or the wealthy. Bulgogi consists of thin slices of sirloin or other top cuts of beef that has been marinated to increase flavor and tenderness.

Cool Fact: Pureed pears are often used as a tenderizer.

Bulgogi

Jajangmyeon is actually a Korean-Chinese noodle dish. During the Joseon Dynasty, the Incheon port opened and many Chinese people immigrated to Korea. The Chinese created restaurants in this region and reinvented a traditional noodle dish more suited for the local Korean taste. Jajangmyeon was a dish created by the working class and was an inexpensive dish for all. To adapt the dish to Korean taste the sauce was changed to make it sweeter by adding caramel. Jajangmyeon is made of a thick, handmade wheat noodle with a sauce mad1e of a dark soybean, caramel paste. Jajangmyeon is traditionally served with a small amount of daikon radish, raw onions and chunjang sauce.

Jajangmyeon

Another dish from the Joseon Dynasty, Japchae, was first made for one of the reigning king's parties. The king liked the dish so much that he promoted the creator to a position equivalent to Secretary of the Treasury. Japchae translates to vegetables mixed and stirred and was originally only made with vegetables. Sweet potato noodles (also known as cellophane noodles) were added to the dish in the early 20th century and have been a mainstay of the dish since. Now Japchae is most commonly served as a side dish at Korean parties and special occasions.

Japchae

Now for dessert! Patbingsu is a shaved ice dessert that can be topped with fruit, condensed milk, fruit syrup and/or red bean paste. Translating to red beans with ice, patbingsu was created during the Joseon Dynasty as officials were seen having crushed ice with various fruits. Now, patbingsu comes in a variety of forms including coffee, yogurt, mango, etc. A version we recommend trying is green tea!
